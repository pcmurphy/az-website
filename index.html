I am a graduate student in the School of Geography and Development at the University of Arizona. I am currently pursuing my Master's of Arts with specific interests in ecophysiology and phytogeography. I came to Tucson with an interest in working with Dr. Greg Barron-Gafford, and it is with the resources and assistance of his lab group that I am able to explore my thesis project.

--

Before moving to Arizona, I received my Bachelor's of Science in Environmental Sciences at San Diego State University. While an undergraduate, the Environmental Sciences program allowed me flexibility in what topics I wanted to explore beyond the classroom. I was fortunate to develop a relationship with faculty in the Field Stations Program, who exposed me to field methods in ecology and watershed management.

In my sophomore year at SDSU, I became a field technician with the Global Change Research Group, led by Dr. Walter Oechel. Working with the GCRG was an excellent opportunity to surround myself with scientific exploration and new techniques in observing ecosystem processes. By the time I finished my degree in 2013, I had taken four trips to the Arctic to maintain and repair long-term eddy covariance systems operated by our lab group.

After graduating, Dr. Oechel (along with Dr. Donatella Zona) agreed to hire me as the full-time lead technician for the GCRG. I worked between Alaska and San Diego for two years (making six more trips to the Arctic in that period), and during that time I was responsible for:

  • Managing all of the field work done by members of our lab group and visiting researchers.
  • Regularly organizing and carrying out multi-day excursions to remote research sites to ensure that instruments were working correctly.
  • The development of new study sites and a new method of obtaining accurate, reliable CO2 and CH4 fluxes from the tundra.
  • Assisting with several graduate student projects across Southern California.

    • --

    Despite my involvement in the Arctic, I had a desire to focus my interests on semi-arid ecosystems similar to those I grew up in. Southern Arizona provides an excellent living laboratory to study ecosystem dynamics in a stressed environment. I am currently investigating how small variations in topography control primary production in a mixed conifer forest. My research should feed into work done by investigators in the Critical Zone Observatories program.
